Ads Control Tower
A structured, hierarchical Google Ads optimization engine that diagnoses problems, takes action within guardrails, and flags what needs human attention — so you manage by exception, not by exhaustion.
The Problem
Most Google Ads accounts are managed with hundreds of independent rules that fire blindly — generating noise, conflicting recommendations, and wasted time. A rule that makes sense for Manual CPC is meaningless for tCPA. A keyword-level flag is irrelevant if the campaign budget is wrong. Without structure, optimization becomes chaos.
The ACT Solution
ACT replaces independent rules with a hierarchical decision engine. Each level of the Google Ads account has a clear job, clear boundaries, and clear actions. Account level allocates budget. Campaign level adjusts strategy. Keyword level manages the details. Every action is categorised, every threshold is explicit, every change has guardrails.

Within-Role Budget Competition
how budget splits when multiple campaigns share a role

When multiple campaigns share the same role (e.g., 3× [CP] Shopping campaigns), ACT must decide how to split that role's budget allocation between them. Budget is ranked by efficiency — the campaign with the best CPA (Lead Gen) or best ROAS (Ecommerce) gets priority. The rules differ based on how many campaigns share the role, with caps to prevent any single campaign from starving the others. These caps are designed to prevent a death spiral where reduced budget leads to less data, worse performance, and even less budget.

1 Campaign in Role
Gets 100% of the role's allocation
No competition. The single campaign receives the entire budget band for that role.
E.g., 1× [BD] Brand campaign → gets the full 2-8% brand budget.
2 Campaigns in Role
Best performer gets up to 65%, other gets min 35%
The better performing campaign gets the larger share. But the weaker campaign still gets at least 35% to maintain its ability to compete and recover.
E.g., 2× [CP] Shopping campaigns → best ROAS gets 65%, other gets 35% of the CP band.
3+ Campaigns in Role
Best performer capped at 50%, rest distributed by efficiency
Prevents one campaign from starving the others. Budget distributed proportionally by efficiency score, with a 50% cap. Ensures underperformers get enough budget to be evaluated fairly.
E.g., 3× [CP] Shopping → best gets 50%, second gets 30%, third gets 20%.
Why the cap matters: A campaign having a bad week shouldn't be starved of budget. Less budget = less data = worse performance = even less budget. The minimum share prevents this death spiral.

Signal Detection Windows
each signal has its own lookback period

Each input signal has its own detection window — how far back ACT looks to determine if that specific signal has changed significantly. More volatile signals get longer windows to avoid false alarms and knee-jerk reactions. These windows are used for signal detection (diagnosing what changed), which is separate from the performance scoring windows (7d/14d/30d weighted blend used for ranking campaigns for budget allocation). Signal detection answers "has this signal changed?" while performance scoring answers "how is this campaign performing overall?"

Changes fast due to auction dynamics and competitor activity. Needs to be caught early before budget is wasted on expensive clicks.
🔄

14-day window
Noisy day-to-day. A single day with 0 conversions doesn't mean CVR has dropped — needs smoothing over 2 weeks.
🛒
Avg Order Value
30-day window
Most volatile signal. One large order can skew a whole week. 30 days smooths out anomalies. Ecommerce only.

Performance Scoring — Multi-Window Blend
7d / 14d / 30d weighted — ranks campaigns for budget

ACT rates each campaign's overall health using a weighted blend of three time windows: 7 days (50% weight), 14 days (30% weight), and 30 days (20% weight). This score determines budget allocation ranking within a campaign role — when multiple campaigns share a role, the campaign with the best performance score gets priority for budget increases. Recent data is weighted most heavily (50% for 7 days) because it is most actionable, but longer-term performance provides stability and prevents overreaction to short-term fluctuations. This scoring is separate from signal detection windows, which diagnose what changed.

Cooldown Periods — Summary
minimum wait between budget adjustments

After ACT adjusts a campaign's budget, it waits for the cooldown period before considering another budget adjustment on that same campaign. This prevents stacking blind changes before the first one has had time to affect campaign delivery. The cooldown resets after each adjustment. The cooldown is editable per client in the Client Configuration tab.

Account level has only one cooldown because account level has only one lever: budget allocation between campaigns. All other cooldowns (keyword bids, device/geo/schedule modifiers, tCPA/tROAS targets, CPC caps) are defined at Campaign Level.

Guardrails & Safety Limits
hard limits ACT cannot breach

Hard limits that ACT cannot breach without human approval at account level. These protect against bad automated decisions. All values are editable per client in the Client Configuration tab. These guardrails apply to account-level budget allocation decisions only — campaign-level guardrails are defined separately on the Campaign Level page.

  • Budget bands: ACT cannot move a campaign role below its minimum or above its maximum allocation percentage.
  • Max single move: Maximum 10% of daily budget can be shifted in a single overnight cycle. Larger moves are queued for human approval before execution.
  • Within-role cap: No single campaign gets more than 50% of its role allocation (3+ campaigns) or 65% (2 campaigns). Solo campaigns get 100%.
  • Testing campaigns [TS]: Budget is never auto-increased. Fixed cap set during account setup.
  • Whole account underperformance: If ALL campaigns are outside target range, ACT flags an Account Health Alert and does NOT redistribute — human must investigate externally.
  • AOV-driven outperformance: For ecommerce accounts, if ROAS outperformance is driven solely by AOV increase, ACT monitors but does NOT auto-scale. Waits for 30-day AOV window to confirm.
  • Budget shift cooldown: After ACT adjusts a campaign's budget, it will not adjust that same campaign's budget again for 72 hours. This gives the change time to affect delivery. The cooldown applies per campaign — ACT can still adjust other campaigns during this period.

Campaign Level Architecture
two layers — universal + strategy-specific

Campaign level has two layers of rules. Universal levers apply to most strategies. Strategy-specific levers only apply to that bid strategy type.

Universal principle across all strategies: Loosening (accepting higher costs or lower returns) always requires approval. Tightening (improving efficiency) can auto-execute.

2. Device adjustments

4. Ad schedule adjustments
5. Match type changes
Strategy-Specific Levers
Unique to each bid strategy:

Actions & Monitoring — Campaign Level
4 response categories for all campaign-level decisions

Every campaign-level recommendation falls into one of four categories. These categories determine how the recommendation is colour-coded across all tables, decision trees, and flowcharts in this section.

✅

Act

ACT auto-executes this change overnight. The issue is inside the ads platform and within ACT's control.
  • • Keyword bid adjustments
  • • Product group bid adjustments
  • • Device/Geo/Schedule modifiers
  • • Tightening tCPA/tROAS targets
  • • Lowering Max Clicks CPC cap
  • • Adding negative keywords
  • • Pausing dead keywords (0 impressions 60+ days)
Executed overnight within guardrails. Reviewed in morning.
📊

Monitor

No action needed. Performance is within target range or there is insufficient data to act on.
  • • CPA/ROAS within target range
  • • Learning period active (hands off)
  • • Already at max impression share
  • • Insufficient data (under click minimums)
  • • CPA fluctuating normally (Max Conversions)
  • • No issues detected (PMax)
No action taken. ACT continues monitoring.
🔍

Investigate

Requires human approval before execution. The fix may be partially outside the ads platform, or the action is a loosening change.
  • • Loosening tCPA/tROAS targets
  • • Raising Max Clicks CPC cap
  • • CVR drops (check search terms, then site)
  • • Match type changes
  • • Pausing keywords with conversion history
  • • All PMax changes
Flagged for morning review with diagnostic data.
🚨

Alert

Urgent issue or hard limit reached. No automated action possible — human must investigate externally or approve an override.
  • • CPC floor or ceiling reached
  • • tCPA/tROAS floor or ceiling reached
  • • 7-day bid cap hit, signal persists
  • • CPA spiking (Max Conversions)
  • • Feed quality issues (Shopping)
  • • Product disapprovals (Merchant Center)
No automated action. Notification only.

Campaign-level actions follow the same overnight execution and morning review flow as account-level budget changes. ACT evaluates each campaign based on its bid strategy type, checks all applicable universal levers, and identifies required actions. Actions marked "Auto" that fall within guardrail limits are executed overnight. Loosening actions and anything exceeding caps are queued for morning approval.

all strategies except PMax · no cooldown

Universal — Match Type Changes
all strategies except PMax · always approval · no cooldown

Match type changes fundamentally alter what traffic a keyword captures. Applies to all bid strategies except PMax. Every match type change requires human approval — ACT never auto-changes a match type. There is no cooldown because match type changes are typically one-way decisions that are rarely reversed. ACT flags broad match keywords with 40%+ irrelevant search terms over 14+ days for downgrade to phrase match. ACT flags exact match keywords converting well but with fewer than 50 impressions per day over 21+ days for potential phrase match expansion to increase volume.

Manual CPC — Universal Levers Only
universal levers only — keyword bids managed at Keyword Level

Manual CPC is the only strategy where individual keyword bids can be directly controlled. However, keyword bid management is handled at Keyword Level, not Campaign Level. At Campaign Level, Manual CPC campaigns use only the universal levers: device adjustments, geo adjustments, ad schedule adjustments, negative keywords, and match type changes. All individual keyword bid adjustments, keyword performance monitoring, search term mining, quality score monitoring, keyword pause recommendations, and keyword conflict detection are managed at Keyword Level.

Keyword bid management is at Keyword Level. Campaign Level controls campaign-wide settings (device, geo, schedule modifiers). Keyword Level controls individual keyword bids, performance monitoring, search term mining, and pause recommendations. This separation applies to Manual CPC only — all other bid strategies do not have keyword-level bid control.

Target CPA — Target Management
limited control · 14-day cooldown · 10% per cycle

Google controls bids per-auction. ACT manages the tCPA target and the environment around it. Universal levers (negatives, device, geo, schedule, match types) also apply to tCPA campaigns. Do NOT adjust individual keyword bids — Google ignores them. ACT can lower the tCPA target by 10% (tightening efficiency, auto-execute) or flag to raise the tCPA target by 10% (loosening, requires approval). Maximum 10% target change per cycle with a 14-day cooldown between changes. ACT never changes the target during a learning period (when the campaign shows "Learning" status or when a target was changed within the last 14 days). tCPA floor and ceiling from Client Configuration apply — ACT never sets a target outside the client's defined range.

Why does ACT raise the tCPA target when CPA is too high?

The tCPA target is a constraint on Google's bidding, not a bid itself. When the target is set too low for the market, Google stops entering auctions because it can't find conversions at that price. The campaign goes quiet, volume drops, and performance gets worse.

Raising the target slightly gives the algorithm room to compete in more auctions. More auctions = more data = better optimisation. Often the actual CPA settles below the new target once volume recovers.

Simple version: tCPA target too tight = Google gives up. Loosen it slightly and performance often improves.
Caps: Max 10% target change per cycle. 14-day cooldown. tCPA floor and ceiling from Client Configuration apply. Loosening always requires approval. Tightening auto-executes.

Maximize Conversions — Monitoring Only
no bids or targets · budget is account level's job

Google tries to get as many conversions as possible for whatever budget is allocated. There is no CPA target and no efficiency constraint — Google will spend the full daily budget every day regardless of CPA. This means CPA can fluctuate significantly. ACT has no bids or targets to adjust on this strategy. Budget allocation is handled at account level, not campaign level. Universal levers (negatives, device, geo, schedule, match types) also apply to Maximize Conversions campaigns. ACT's primary job on this strategy is monitoring CPA and recommending when to graduate to tCPA. If CPA has been stable for 21+ days, ACT flags a recommendation to switch to tCPA at the stable CPA level. If CPA spikes 30%+ in 7 days, ACT raises an alert about potential low-quality conversions.

Max Clicks — CPC Cap Management
with or without CPC cap · 7-day cooldown · 10% per cycle

Google gets as many clicks as possible for whatever budget is allocated. Comes in two variants — with and without a max CPC cap. Budget allocation is handled at account level, not campaign level. Universal levers (negatives, device, geo, schedule, match types) also apply to Max Clicks campaigns. Without a CPC cap, Google bids freely with no ceiling — ACT monitors CPC trends and recommends adding a cap if CPC becomes volatile. With a CPC cap, ACT can lower the cap by 10% (tightening, auto-execute) or flag to raise the cap by 10% (loosening, requires approval). CPC cap changes have a 7-day cooldown. For both variants, if the campaign accumulates 30+ conversions in 30 days, ACT flags a recommendation to switch to a conversion-based strategy (Maximize Conversions or tCPA).

Guardrails & Safety Limits — Campaign Level
hard limits ACT cannot breach

Hard limits that ACT cannot breach without human approval at campaign level. All values are editable per client in the Client Configuration tab.

  • Loosening always requires approval: Any action that accepts higher costs or lower returns (raising tCPA target, lowering tROAS target, raising Max Clicks CPC cap) always requires human approval before execution.
  • Tightening can auto-execute: Any action that improves efficiency (lowering tCPA target, raising tROAS target, lowering Max Clicks CPC cap) can be auto-executed within the defined caps.
  • Per-cycle max 10%: No single lever can move more than 10% in one overnight cycle. Applies to keyword bids, product group bids, device/geo/schedule modifiers, tCPA/tROAS targets, and Max Clicks CPC caps.
  • 7-day max bid change 30%: Keyword bids and product group bids cannot move more than 30% total in any 7-day period. If the cap is hit and the signal persists, ACT flags for human approval.
  • CPC floor and ceiling: ACT never bids below the CPC floor or above the CPC ceiling defined in Client Configuration. Applies to Manual CPC keyword bids and Standard Shopping product group bids.
  • tCPA/tROAS floor and ceiling: ACT never sets a tCPA target below the floor or above the ceiling, and never sets a tROAS target below the floor or above the ceiling, as defined in Client Configuration.
  • Device modifier cap: Auto-execution limited to -60% (negative) and +30% (positive). Beyond these limits requires approval. Full device exclusion (-100%) always requires approval.
  • Geo modifier cap: Auto-execution limited to -50% (negative) and +30% (positive). Beyond -50% up to -90% requires approval. Full location exclusion always requires approval.
  • Ad schedule modifier cap: Auto-execution limited to -50% (negative) and +25% (positive). Beyond these limits requires approval. Pausing a time slot entirely always requires approval.
  • Never change targets during learning: ACT never adjusts tCPA or tROAS targets when the campaign shows "Learning" status or when a target was changed within the last 14 days.
  • Match type changes always require approval: ACT never auto-changes a keyword's match type. Every match type change is flagged for human review.
  • Keyword pausing requires approval: ACT can auto-lower bids on non-converting keywords, but pausing a keyword always requires human approval (except dead keywords with 0 impressions for 60+ days).
  • Product exclusions require approval: Excluding a product from Standard Shopping always requires human approval.
  • All PMax changes require approval: PMax has very limited levers and consequences are hard to predict. Brand exclusions, asset changes, audience signal changes, and target adjustments all require human approval.

Guardrails & Safety Limits — Keyword Level
hard limits for keyword-level actions

Hard limits that ACT cannot breach at keyword level. Keyword-level guardrails protect against excessive bid changes, premature keyword pausing, and unauthorized keyword additions. All values are editable per client in the Client Configuration tab.

  • Bid adjustment per-cycle max 10%: No single keyword bid can change more than 10% in one overnight cycle. Applies to Manual CPC campaigns only.
  • 7-day max bid change 30%: No keyword bid can move more than 30% total in any 7-day period. If the cap is hit and the signal persists, ACT flags for human approval. Applies to Manual CPC campaigns only.
  • CPC floor and ceiling: ACT never bids below the CPC floor or above the CPC ceiling defined in Client Configuration. Applies to Manual CPC campaigns only.
  • Auto-pause threshold: Keywords are auto-paused only after spending 1x CPA target with 0 conversions over 14+ days, or after 0 impressions for 60+ days. ACT never auto-pauses a keyword that has conversion history.
  • Converting keyword pause requires approval: A keyword that is converting (even at high CPA) can only be paused with human approval. ACT flags it but does not auto-pause.
  • New keyword addition always requires approval: ACT never auto-adds new keywords to the account. All keyword discovery suggestions require human review and approval.
  • New keywords always added as phrase match: When adding discovered search terms as keywords, they are always added as phrase match. Never broad match, never exact match initially.
  • Negative keyword auto-add limited to zero-converting and irrelevant patterns: ACT only auto-adds negatives for terms with spend > 1x target and 0 conversions (14+ days), and for clearly irrelevant patterns. Converting terms with high CPA require approval before blocking.

Guardrails & Safety Limits — Ad Level
hard limits for ad-level actions

Hard limits that apply at ad level. Since this level has no auto-executed actions, the guardrails focus on what ACT must never do automatically and what context checks must pass before flagging.

  • ACT never writes or modifies ad copy: All ad creative changes (headlines, descriptions, RSA assets) require human action. ACT can only flag issues and recommend improvements.
  • ACT never creates or modifies extensions: All extension creation (sitelinks, callouts, structured snippets, call extensions) requires human action. ACT can only flag when extensions are missing or insufficient.
  • ACT never pauses or removes ads: Even if an ad is the worst performer, ACT flags it for review but does not auto-pause. The decision to pause or replace an ad is always made by a human.
  • Ads under 14 days are skipped in comparisons: ACT checks the ad creation date and excludes any ad live for fewer than 14 days from performance comparisons. New ads need time to bed in before being judged.
  • Performance comparison requires sufficient data: ACT only flags a worst performer if it has 100+ impressions and has been live for 21+ days. Insufficient data means no flag.
  • All ad-level flags require approval: There are no auto-executed actions at ad level. Every flag, recommendation, and alert requires human review and approval.
  • Extensions checked at both levels: ACT checks extension coverage at both campaign level and ad group level. Missing extensions at either level are flagged separately.
  • Call extension check requires phone tracking context: ACT only flags missing call extensions if the client has phone call tracking configured. If no phone tracking, call extension is not flagged as missing.

5. These are the same shared account-level lists used by Search campaigns — negatives apply across all campaign types.

Guardrails & Safety Limits — Shopping Level
hard limits for Shopping-level actions

Hard limits that ACT cannot breach at Shopping level. Product exclusions always require approval. Product group bid adjustments are capped at 10% per cycle, 72-hour cooldown per product group, 30% maximum in any 7-day period, with CPC floor and ceiling from Client Configuration. All values are editable per client in the Client Configuration tab.

  • ACT never auto-excludes products: All product exclusion recommendations require human approval. ACT flags products for exclusion but the decision is always made by a human.
  • Product group bid per-cycle max 10%: No product group bid can change more than 10% in one overnight cycle.
  • 7-day max bid change 30%: No product group bid can move more than 30% total in any 7-day period. If the cap is hit and the signal persists, ACT flags for human approval.
  • CPC floor and ceiling: ACT never bids below the CPC floor or above the CPC ceiling defined in Client Configuration for Shopping product groups.
  • 72-hour bid cooldown: After ACT adjusts a product group bid, it will not adjust that same product group again for 72 hours.
  • Configurable spend threshold for losers: The spend threshold that defines a "loser" product (spend with 0 conversions) is a configurable absolute amount set per client in Client Configuration (default: £50). ACT does not use a formula — the client decides what "significant spend" means for their products.
  • Tier percentages are editable: The default "top 20%" for best sellers can be adjusted per client. This accounts for different catalogue sizes — a client with 10,000 products might use top 10%, a client with 50 products might use top 30%.
  • Standard Shopping only: All Shopping-level rules apply to Standard Shopping campaigns only. PMax Shopping is managed separately at Campaign Level and is not affected by Shopping-level rules.

Client Onboarding Checklist
what ACT sets up automatically when a new client is added

When a new client is onboarded into ACT, the following are set up automatically. The account manager configures the editable settings above, then ACT handles the structural setup below.

  • 9 Negative Keyword Lists: ACT auto-creates 9 account-level shared negative keyword lists organised by word count and match type (1 WORD "phrase", 1 WORD [exact], 2 WORDS "phrase", 2 WORDS [exact], 3 WORDS "phrase", 3 WORDS [exact], 4 WORDS "phrase", 4 WORDS [exact], 5+ WORDS [exact]). Same structure for every client.
  • Campaign Role Detection: ACT scans campaign names for role prefixes ([BD], [CP], [RT], [PR], [TS]) and maps them automatically. Account manager verifies the mapping is correct.
  • Default Settings Applied: All settings above are initialised with their default values. Account manager reviews and overrides as needed per client.
  • Overnight Monitoring Activated: ACT begins running overnight evaluations across all levels (Account, Campaign, Ad Group, Keyword, Ad, Shopping) from the first night after onboarding.
