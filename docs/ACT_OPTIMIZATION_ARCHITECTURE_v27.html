Within-Role Budget Competition
how budget splits when multiple campaigns share a role

When multiple campaigns share the same role (e.g., 3× [CP] Shopping campaigns), ACT must decide how to split that role's budget allocation between them. Budget is ranked by efficiency — the campaign with the best CPA (Lead Gen) or best ROAS (Ecommerce) gets priority. The rules differ based on how many campaigns share the role, with caps to prevent any single campaign from starving the others. These caps are designed to prevent a death spiral where reduced budget leads to less data, worse performance, and even less budget.

1 Campaign in Role
Gets 100% of the role's allocation
No competition. The single campaign receives the entire budget band for that role.
E.g., 1× [BD] Brand campaign → gets the full 2-8% brand budget.
2 Campaigns in Role
Best performer gets up to 65%, other gets min 35%
The better performing campaign gets the larger share. But the weaker campaign still gets at least 35% to maintain its ability to compete and recover.
E.g., 2× [CP] Shopping campaigns → best ROAS gets 65%, other gets 35% of the CP band.
3+ Campaigns in Role
Best performer capped at 50%, rest distributed by efficiency
Prevents one campaign from starving the others. Budget distributed proportionally by efficiency score, with a 50% cap. Ensures underperformers get enough budget to be evaluated fairly.
E.g., 3× [CP] Shopping → best gets 50%, second gets 30%, third gets 20%.
Why the cap matters: A campaign having a bad week shouldn't be starved of budget. Less budget = less data = worse performance = even less budget. The minimum share prevents this death spiral.

When CPA moves outside the target range, ACT checks which signal moved to determine the correct response. Budget shift actions have a 72-hour cooldown per campaign. Signal Detection Windows
each signal has its own lookback period

Each input signal has its own detection window — how far back ACT looks to determine if that specific signal has changed significantly. More volatile signals get longer windows to avoid false alarms and knee-jerk reactions. These windows are used for signal detection (diagnosing what changed), which is separate from the performance scoring windows (7d/14d/30d weighted blend used for ranking campaigns for budget allocation). Signal detection answers "has this signal changed?" while performance scoring answers "how is this campaign performing overall?"

🔄

🛒
Avg Order Value
30-day window

Performance Scoring — Multi-Window Blend
7d / 14d / 30d weighted — ranks campaigns for budget

ACT rates each campaign's overall health using a weighted blend of three time windows: 7 days (50% weight), 14 days (30% weight), and 30 days (20% weight). This score determines budget allocation ranking within a campaign role — when multiple campaigns share a role, the campaign with the best performance score gets priority for budget increases. Recent data is weighted most heavily (50% for 7 days) because it is most actionable, but longer-term performance provides stability and prevents overreaction to short-term fluctuations. This scoring is separate from signal detection windows, which diagnose what changed.

7 Days — 50% Weight

30 Days — 20% Weight

How scoring and signal detection work together: Signal detection (CPC 7d, CVR 14d, AOV 30d) tells ACT what's wrong (diagnosis). Performance scoring (7/14/30 blend) tells ACT how to rank campaigns for budget allocation (prioritisation). Both run in parallel — different jobs, complementary outputs.

Cooldown Periods — Summary
minimum wait between budget adjustments

After ACT adjusts a campaign's budget, it waits for the cooldown period before considering another budget adjustment on that same campaign. This prevents stacking blind changes before the first one has had time to affect campaign delivery. The cooldown resets after each adjustment. The cooldown is editable per client in the Client Configuration tab.

Guardrails & Safety Limits
hard limits ACT cannot breach

Hard limits that ACT cannot breach without human approval at account level. These protect against bad automated decisions. All values are editable per client in the Client Configuration tab. These guardrails apply to account-level budget allocation decisions only — campaign-level guardrails are defined separately on the Campaign Level page.

  • Budget bands: ACT cannot move a campaign role below its minimum or above its maximum allocation percentage.
  • Max single move: Maximum 10% of daily budget can be shifted in a single overnight cycle. Larger moves are queued for human approval before execution.
  • Within-role cap: No single campaign gets more than 50% of its role allocation (3+ campaigns) or 65% (2 campaigns). Solo campaigns get 100%.
  • Testing campaigns [TS]: Budget is never auto-increased. Fixed cap set during account setup.
  • Whole account underperformance: If ALL campaigns are outside target range, ACT flags an Account Health Alert and does NOT redistribute — human must investigate externally.
  • AOV-driven outperformance: For ecommerce accounts, if ROAS outperformance is driven solely by AOV increase, ACT monitors but does NOT auto-scale. Waits for 30-day AOV window to confirm.
  • Budget shift cooldown: After ACT adjusts a campaign's budget, it will not adjust that same campaign's budget again for 72 hours. This gives the change time to affect delivery. The cooldown applies per campaign — ACT can still adjust other campaigns during this period.

Universal — Negative Keywords
all strategies except PMax · no cooldown

The single most impactful universal lever. Applies to all bid strategies except PMax. Bad search terms waste budget regardless of bid strategy. Negative keywords are a binary action — either a term is blocked or it isn't. There is no cooldown period because there is no gradual adjustment to evaluate. ACT auto-adds negatives for terms with spend above 1x CPA target and 0 conversions over 14+ days, and for clearly irrelevant patterns. Converting terms with CPA 30%+ above target require human approval before blocking, as they may still have value.

Why does ACT raise the tCPA target when CPA is too high?

The tCPA target is a constraint on Google's bidding, not a bid itself. When the target is set too low for the market, Google stops entering auctions because it can't find conversions at that price. The campaign goes quiet, volume drops, and performance gets worse.

Raising the target slightly gives the algorithm room to compete in more auctions. More auctions = more data = better optimisation. Often the actual CPA settles below the new target once volume recovers.

Simple version: tCPA target too tight = Google gives up. Loosen it slightly and performance often improves.
Caps: Max 10% target change per cycle. 14-day cooldown. tCPA floor and ceiling from Client Configuration apply. Loosening always requires approval. Tightening auto-executes.

Guardrails & Safety Limits — Campaign Level
hard limits ACT cannot breach

Hard limits that ACT cannot breach without human approval at campaign level. All values are editable per client in the Client Configuration tab.

  • Loosening always requires approval: Any action that accepts higher costs or lower returns (raising tCPA target, lowering tROAS target, raising Max Clicks CPC cap) always requires human approval before execution.
  • Tightening can auto-execute: Any action that improves efficiency (lowering tCPA target, raising tROAS target, lowering Max Clicks CPC cap) can be auto-executed within the defined caps.
  • Per-cycle max 10%: No single lever can move more than 10% in one overnight cycle. Applies to keyword bids, product group bids, device/geo/schedule modifiers, tCPA/tROAS targets, and Max Clicks CPC caps.
  • 7-day max bid change 30%: Keyword bids and product group bids cannot move more than 30% total in any 7-day period. If the cap is hit and the signal persists, ACT flags for human approval.
  • CPC floor and ceiling: ACT never bids below the CPC floor or above the CPC ceiling defined in Client Configuration. Applies to Manual CPC keyword bids and Standard Shopping product group bids.
  • tCPA/tROAS floor and ceiling: ACT never sets a tCPA target below the floor or above the ceiling, and never sets a tROAS target below the floor or above the ceiling, as defined in Client Configuration.
  • Device modifier cap: Auto-execution limited to -60% (negative) and +30% (positive). Beyond these limits requires approval. Full device exclusion (-100%) always requires approval.
  • Geo modifier cap: Auto-execution limited to -50% (negative) and +30% (positive). Beyond -50% up to -90% requires approval. Full location exclusion always requires approval.
  • Ad schedule modifier cap: Auto-execution limited to -50% (negative) and +25% (positive). Beyond these limits requires approval. Pausing a time slot entirely always requires approval.
  • Never change targets during learning: ACT never adjusts tCPA or tROAS targets when the campaign shows "Learning" status or when a target was changed within the last 14 days.
  • Match type changes always require approval: ACT never auto-changes a keyword's match type. Every match type change is flagged for human review.
  • Keyword pausing requires approval: ACT can auto-lower bids on non-converting keywords, but pausing a keyword always requires human approval (except dead keywords with 0 impressions for 60+ days).
  • Product exclusions require approval: Excluding a product from Standard Shopping always requires human approval.
  • All PMax changes require approval: PMax has very limited levers and consequences are hard to predict. Brand exclusions, asset changes, audience signal changes, and target adjustments all require human approval.

monitoring and flagging — minimal auto-execution

Ad group level receives context from campaign level (bid strategy, budget allocation, performance targets) and monitors how individual ad groups perform within each campaign. Unlike campaign level which has multiple levers to pull, ad group level is primarily about detecting problems and opportunities that require human attention. There are no bid strategy distinctions at this level — the rules apply regardless of whether the campaign uses Manual CPC, tCPA, tROAS, or any other strategy. Quality score is not monitored at this level — it sits at keyword level.

What ad group level does: Detects performance outliers (both negative and positive), flags budget concentration issues, identifies structural problems, and recommends when an outperforming ad group should be promoted to a standalone campaign. Almost all actions require human approval — ACT does not auto-pause or auto-restructure ad groups.

Guardrails & Safety Limits — Ad Group Level
hard limits for ad group level

Hard limits that apply at ad group level. Since this level has no auto-executed actions, the guardrails focus on what ACT must never do automatically and what context checks must pass before flagging.

  • ACT never auto-pauses an ad group: Pausing an ad group affects all keywords and ads inside it. This is too impactful to automate. ACT can flag an ad group for pause consideration, but the decision is always made by a human.
  • ACT never auto-restructures ad groups: Moving keywords between ad groups, splitting ad groups, or merging ad groups always requires human decision-making.
  • Budget concentration context check: ACT must count active (enabled, not paused) ad groups before flagging budget concentration. If only 1 ad group is active, concentration is expected and ACT does not flag it.
  • Positive outlier promotion is a suggestion only: Recommending that an ad group be promoted to a standalone campaign is always a suggestion requiring human approval. ACT never auto-creates new campaigns.
  • All ad group flags require approval: There are no auto-executed actions at ad group level. Every flag, recommendation, and alert requires human review and approval before any action is taken.
